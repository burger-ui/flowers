Our Story

Flowers that have
shaped history

From the Indigenous peoples of North America who cultivated sunflowers for sustenance, to the Dutch 'tulipmania' of the 1630s — these two flowers have remarkable tales woven into human civilization.

🌻
Sunflower
Helianthus annuus
  • Native to North America; cultivated for 3,000+ years
  • Symbol of warmth, adoration, and loyalty
  • Grows best in full sun with well-drained, rich soil
  • Used for edible seeds, culinary oil, and bird feed
  • Plant after last frost; 1–2 inches deep
🌷
Tulip
Tulipa spp.
  • Originated in Central Asia; beloved by the Ottoman Empire
  • Symbol of love, rebirth, and charity
  • Plant bulbs 6 inches deep in autumn
  • Over 3,000 registered cultivars worldwide
  • Deadhead spent blooms for stronger bulbs next year
Timeline

A history
of blooms

3000 BCE
Indigenous peoples of North America first cultivate sunflowers for food, oil, and dye.
10th century
Tulips grow wild across Central Asia; Ottoman sultans begin formal cultivation in palace gardens.
1500s
Spanish conquistadors bring sunflowers to Europe; they quickly become an ornamental sensation.
1630s
'Tulipmania' sweeps the Netherlands — single bulbs sell for more than a craftsman's annual salary.
1880
Vincent van Gogh paints his iconic Sunflowers series, cementing the flower in art history forever.
Today
Over 3,000 tulip cultivars registered; sunflowers are among the world's top 5 oilseed crops.
